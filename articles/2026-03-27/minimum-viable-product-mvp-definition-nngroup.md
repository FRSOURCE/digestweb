## Summary & Key Takeaways

- The article defines a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) as the smallest possible product that can be released to gather validated learning about users.
- MVPs are primarily learning tools designed to test whether a product idea holds value for its target audience.
- The core purpose of an MVP is to minimize risk and maximize learning by getting real user feedback early in the development cycle.
- It emphasizes that an MVP is not a half-finished product but a complete, albeit minimal, solution to a core problem.
- NNGroup highlights that a successful MVP provides enough value to attract early adopters and demonstrate future benefits.

## Our Commentary

It's easy to forget the "viable" part of MVP, isn't it? We often see products launched that are barely functional, let alone viable. NNGROUP's emphasis on MVPs as _learning tools_ is a crucial reminder. It's not just about shipping fast; it's about shipping smart. I appreciate the clarity here, especially for teams that might be conflating MVP with a quick-and-dirty prototype. This article serves as a good refresher for seasoned pros and an essential guide for newcomers navigating the product development landscape.
